Smoke Testing vs. Sanity Testing: Wide and Shallow vs. Narrow and Deep

Both of these are quick — a matter of minutes, not a full test cycle — which is exactly why they get confused for each other. They're checking different things at different moments. Smoke testing asks: is this build stable enough to bother testing at all. Sanity testing asks: does this specific change actually make sense, narrowly and quickly, without a full regression pass.

Smoke testing: is the build even worth testing

Smoke testing runs against a brand-new build, covering the most critical paths across the whole application — can a user log in, does the main page load, do the core flows at least start without immediately erupting into errors. It's intentionally broad and intentionally shallow: the goal isn't to find subtle bugs, it's to catch a build that's fundamentally broken before anyone spends hours running detailed test cases against something that was never stable to begin with.

Sanity testing: does this specific change hold up

Sanity testing happens after a specific fix or minor change, focused narrowly on the affected area and its immediate surroundings — not the whole application, just enough to confirm the change is reasonable and didn't obviously break something adjacent. It's narrower than the regression testing a bigger change would warrant, and it's usually unscripted — a quick, focused check rather than a formal documented pass.

The shape is the real difference

Smoke testing is wide and shallow: touch every major area briefly, go deep on none of them. Sanity testing is narrow and deep-ish: go deep on one specific area, touch nothing outside it. Picturing it that way makes the confusion easier to avoid — asking "is this smoke or sanity" is really asking "am I checking breadth across the whole build, or depth on one specific change."

A simple trigger to remember which applies: a new build lands → smoke test first, before anything else. A small fix or minor change ships → sanity test that specific area. If both a new build and a batch of fixes arrive together, both apply, in that order — smoke first to confirm the build is worth testing at all, then sanity checks on the individual changes within it.

Neither replaces a full test cycle, and neither should be mistaken for one. They exist to answer a fast, specific question early — is this worth testing further, and does this one change look right — so that deeper testing effort doesn't get spent on a build or a fix that was obviously broken from the first few minutes.
