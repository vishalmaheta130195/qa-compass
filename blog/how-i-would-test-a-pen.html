How I'd Test a Pen: A Test-Scenario Writing Exercise

One of the oldest exercises in QA training is also one of the best: forget the app for a minute, and write test scenarios for something completely ordinary — a pen. No login screen, no API, no edge cases involving Unicode. Just a plain ballpoint pen. It sounds almost too simple to matter, but it's a surprisingly good way to practice the actual skill of testing: noticing everything a "simple" thing has to get right.

Here's how I'd break it down.

Start with what the product is actually for

A pen's core job is trivial to state and easy to under-test: it has to write, reliably, for as long as it's expected to last. Everything else — the cap, the grip, the ink — exists to support that one job. So the first scenarios are always the obvious ones:

  • Does it write immediately, without skipping, on the first stroke?
  • Does the ink flow consistently across a full page, not just the first few lines?
  • Does the line width stay consistent, or does it thicken and thin unpredictably?

Then test the conditions it's expected to survive

This is where the exercise gets interesting, because it forces you to think about environment and durability — the same category of thinking you'd apply to a mobile app that needs to survive a bad network or a low-battery state:

  • Does it still write after being dropped?
  • Does it survive being carried in a pocket or bag all day without leaking?
  • Does the ink hold up on different paper types — a notebook page, an exam answer sheet, glossy paper?
  • Does performance change in heat, or after long periods unused?

Don't skip usability

A pen that writes perfectly but is miserable to hold still fails its user. This maps directly to UX testing on real products — functional correctness isn't the whole story:

  • Is the grip comfortable for extended writing?
  • Is the cap easy to remove and post back on one-handed?
  • Is the length and balance reasonable for different hand sizes?
Notice the pattern: functional scenarios, durability/environmental scenarios, and usability scenarios. That's the same three-part lens I reach for on any feature I test — does it work, does it hold up under real conditions, and is it pleasant to actually use.

Why this exercise actually works

The value isn't in testing pens for a living — it's that a physical object has no hidden implementation to lean on. You can't "check the code" to see what should happen; you have to reason about intent, environment, and the user entirely from observation. That's precisely the muscle real testing requires, especially with a feature you've never seen before and no spec to fall back on.

Next time you're stuck writing test cases for something and the scenarios feel thin, try the pen exercise on paper first. It's a fast way to remind yourself what "thorough" actually looks like before you go back to the feature that's paying your salary.
